# Related Work

**Humor datasets and shared tasks.** Several shared tasks have produced crowd-labeled humor datasets that we rely on directly. SemEval-2017 Task 6 (Potash et al., 2017) produced a dataset involving pairwise ranking on crowd-voted tweets; SemEval-2020 Task 7 (Hossain et al., 2020) collected crowd funniness ratings for edited news headlines with a pairwise subtask; SemEval-2021 Task 7 (Meaney et al., 2021) rated 10,000 texts with 20 annotators each. Hessel et al. (2023) introduced NYCC, a native pairwise dataset of New Yorker caption contest entries, where individual human agreement on the pairwise task is 64.6%. They also show that fine-tuned models (T5-11B: 65.6%, GPT3-175B: 69.8%) and GPT-4 with few-shot prompting (68.2%) can approach or exceed this level using text descriptions of the cartoon images. All prior work trains and evaluates on individual datasets in isolation. We unify five crowd-labeled datasets spanning text jokes, news headlines, Reddit posts, Spanish tweets, and cartoon captions into one pairwise training collection and study whether joint training across humor domains generalizes.

**Prior approaches to humor judgment.** LLM-as-judge (Zheng et al., 2023) works well for helpfulness evaluation but degrades on subjective and cultural tasks including humor (Verga et al., 2024). Goes et al. (2022) propose simulating crowd judgment by prompting the same LLM with four HSQ humor personality types, but Santurkar et al. (2023) show persona prompts have low steerability and models default to their prior regardless of instruction. Chain-of-thought prompting has been shown to collapse LLM judgment distributions on subjective tasks (Wang et al., 2025). The SemEval 2026 lmfaoooo system scores jokes on 17 humor dimensions pointwise, but without fine-tuning the pointwise variant does not improve over zero-shot. We empirically test all of these approaches and find none breaks the zero-shot plateau for models below GPT-4 scale, motivating direct supervision on crowd-labeled pairwise data via LoRA fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022).
