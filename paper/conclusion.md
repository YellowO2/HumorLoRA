# Conclusion

We systematically evaluated eleven approaches to humor preference judgment, ranging from prompt engineering and persona simulation to activation probing and LoRA fine-tuning. The central finding is that prompting-based approaches consistently plateau at 54–57% across model sizes, prompt variants, and humor domains — a gap we attribute to the absence of crowd-calibrated humor signal in standard pretraining and alignment pipelines, not to prompt design. Crowd-supervised fine-tuning reliably breaks through this plateau, reaching 65–68% on held-out benchmarks and matching what much larger fine-tuned models achieve on NYCC.

The key ingredient is crowd-labeled pairwise data. Individual humor taste is highly subjective, but crowd aggregation recovers a stable, learnable signal that captures the population-level tendency of what people find funny. To make this signal accessible for future work, we assemble and unify five crowd-labeled humor datasets spanning text jokes, news headlines, Reddit posts, Spanish tweets, and cartoon captions into a single pairwise training collection.

The joint LoRA trained on this collection functions as a humor reward model. A natural application is a generation pipeline where a language model produces several candidate replies to a social media post or conversation turn, and the reward model ranks them by predicted funniness — selecting the reply most likely to land with a crowd. We demonstrate this as a live demo, using real Reddit posts as input scenarios.

Future directions include testing at larger model scales, incorporating actual cartoon images for NYCC rather than text descriptions, and cross-lingual evaluation using the Spanish dataset as a held-out benchmark. A longer-term direction is moving beyond crowd annotation entirely, training instead on behavioral signals from real human reactions — laughter and engagement cues from podcasts, videos, or daily conversation, which would capture humor response more directly and at scale without relying on explicit rating tasks.
